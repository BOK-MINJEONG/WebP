Early Stages of Artificial Intelligence

In 1950, British mathematician Alan Turing described in his paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" a test to determine if machines can think, the feasibility of developing intelligent machines, and learning machines. The Turing machine, actualized from these technologies, influenced directly/indirectly John von Neumann, becoming the standard of modern computer architecture and is considered the start of the history of artificial intelligence.

Development of Artificial Intelligence

Since the 1970s, most companies have focused their R&D towards practical statistical techniques. Modern statistics began in the early 20th century, led by scholars like Fisher and Pearson in the UK and Genichi Taguchi in Japan. They showed that experimental design methods and statistical analysis techniques could improve manufacturing quality and production efficiency.
These concepts have been used in the industry as tools to resolve inefficiencies under the name of data mining and have established the foundations of big data technology.

Artificial intelligence research quieted down for a while but spread in earnest in the 1980s with the introduction of expert systems in the industry. Expert systems consist of:

  1. Database of knowledge and experience
  2. Decision-making inference engine
  3. User interface

At that time, inference engine technologies primarily used Bayesian-based probabilistic methods and another approach utilizing multi-value logic through Fuzzy theory. Fuzzy expert systems stood as the representative technology of AI for a while, but as their massive management needs and limited return on investment became apparent, AI research weakened, and most research shifted towards supercomputers and simulation fields in 1993, starting in the USA.
During this dark period for AI, techniques like reinforcement learning for machine control, advocated by Richard Bellman, and statistical methods for quality control, promoted by George Box and other Japanese quality researchers, were applied in industry. Meanwhile, groundbreaking AI research such as backpropagation, the foundation of deep learning, was published but went unnoticed due to computer performance limitations and was replaced by machine learning algorithms.

The Rise of Deep Learning and Machine Learning

By the 2000s, deep learning technologies based on Professor Hinton's Deep Belief Networks began to show practical potential. Particularly, Deep-CNN marked significant achievements in image recognition performance, prompting a reevaluation of neural network-based AI technologies among experts. For instance, after proving the potential of deep learning, Google acquired DeepMind Technologies for $400 million in 2014.
Subsequently, in 2016, AlphaGo 1.0 defeated Lee Sedol, and in 2017, AlphaGo 2.0 (based on unsupervised learning with minimal data self-learning) won against Ke Jie and other top-class Go players, solidifying AI's recognition among the general public and marking a complete reevaluation of the technology.
The steady progress since then has started showing results in other research areas that seemed impractical, and AI has been rapidly advancing since the emergence of deep learning.

The Present and the Future

The development of AI has seen remarkable growth over the past decades, and its prospects for both the present and the future are very bright. Today, artificial intelligence is used in various fields such as natural language processing technologies developed by OpenAI like GPT-3, computer vision technologies used in Apple's Face ID, and autonomous vehicles, and it is expected that AI technologies will become more sophisticated and utilized in all aspects of human life.
